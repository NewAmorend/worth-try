# Research Checklist

Use this checklist when evaluating a real company and current public information may change the recommendation. Search public company facts, not the user's private details.

## Source Hierarchy

Prefer stronger, dated sources:

1. Official filings, investor relations, annual reports, quarterly reports, earnings calls, regulatory notices.
2. Company announcements, product blogs, security or incident pages, leadership pages, job postings.
3. Reputable business and technology news with named authors and dates.
4. Funding databases, industry reports, customer case studies, app-store or marketplace signals, public customer references.
5. Employee-review sites, forums, and social media only as weak signals that need corroboration.

Do not cite search-result snippets as sources. Open the source, check the date, and cite the page used.

## Search Patterns

Use focused searches such as:

- `<company> latest funding revenue layoffs`
- `<company> 2026 layoffs hiring freeze`
- `<company> annual report 10-K 10-Q investor presentation`
- `<company> earnings call margin revenue guidance`
- `<company> security incident data breach`
- `<company> lawsuit regulator investigation`
- `<company> Glassdoor reviews manager workload culture`
- `<company> LinkedIn headcount growth`
- `<company> customers case study`
- `<company> competitors market share`

For startups, add searches for runway, last funding round, lead investors, founder history, board changes, customer concentration, and hiring velocity.

For public companies, inspect recent filings, earnings-call tone, segment growth, margin trend, guidance changes, stock movement only as context, and management commentary.

## Currentness Checks

- Use exact dates for news, filings, funding, layoffs, leadership changes, or financial results.
- Prefer information from the last 12 months for company health, and the last 3-6 months for layoffs, hiring freezes, funding, or leadership churn.
- When sources conflict, state the conflict and prefer primary or more recent sources.
- If the company name is ambiguous, verify location, industry, website, ticker, or legal entity before concluding.

## Evidence to Collect

Company health:

- Revenue growth, profitability, burn, runway, funding quality, debt, customer retention, competitive pressure.
- Layoffs, hiring freezes, reorgs, leadership departures, office closures, or strategy pivots.
- Product momentum, customer wins/losses, market category growth, regulatory or platform dependency.

Role and team:

- Whether the role is tied to a core business priority.
- Hiring manager stability, reporting line, team size, open headcount, on-call or travel expectations.
- Signals from job description changes, repeated reposting, or vague scope.

Offer quality:

- Written compensation terms, bonus mechanics, equity type, strike price, vesting, refreshers, liquidity path.
- Benefits, relocation, immigration, remote policy, start-date pressure, severance, non-compete or clawback terms.

Culture:

- Patterns across employee reviews, not single dramatic anecdotes.
- Look for consistency around manager quality, promotion fairness, workload, ethics, and attrition.
- Treat review averages as noisy; use them to generate questions, not as proof.

## Citation Expectations

In the final answer:

- Cite the most decision-relevant sources, not every opened page.
- Pair each source with the claim it supports.
- Label inference separately from sourced facts.
- Include a confidence rating based on source quality, recency, and how much role-specific information the user provided.
