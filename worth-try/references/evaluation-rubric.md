# Evaluation Rubric

Use this rubric to make offer and company tradeoffs explicit. Adjust weights only when the user states a clear strategy such as stability-first, growth-first, family-first, immigration-first, or cash-first.

## Default Balanced Weights

| Dimension | Weight | What to Evaluate |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Company and business health | 20 | Market, growth, funding or profitability, customer quality, competitive position, leadership credibility, layoffs, runway, public-company financials, startup financing quality, and stage-specific business signals. |
| Role, team, and manager | 20 | Scope, ownership, reporting line, manager quality, team stability, decision rights, technical or functional bar, whether the role is core to the business. |
| Career growth | 20 | Learning density, promotion path, resume signal, network, future options, strategic skill accumulation, proximity to important problems. |
| Compensation and risk-adjusted value | 15 | Base, bonus, equity, vesting, liquidity, benefits, severance, relocation, tax or currency effects, probability-adjusted upside. |
| Culture, workload, and values | 15 | Workload, autonomy, psychological safety, ethics, operating cadence, remote or office expectations, user values fit. |
| Personal constraints | 10 | Location, commute, family, health, visa or immigration, timing, financial runway, life-stage fit, downside tolerance. |

Total weight: 100.

## Scoring

Score each dimension from 1 to 5:

| Score | Meaning |
| ---: | --- |
| 5 | Strong positive evidence; clearly supports taking the offer. |
| 4 | Net positive; some manageable uncertainty. |
| 3 | Mixed or average; decision depends on user priorities. |
| 2 | Concerning; needs mitigation or negotiation. |
| 1 | Major negative or unresolved risk. |
| ? | Insufficient evidence; do not invent a score. |

Weighted score formula: `dimension weight * score / 5`. If a dimension is unknown, show `?`, explain what is missing, and lower confidence rather than pretending it is neutral.

## Recommendation Bands

- 80-100: Strong accept or strong pursue, unless a red-flag cap applies.
- 65-79: Conditional accept or negotiate; good enough if key risks are resolved.
- 50-64: Caution; likely continue interviewing or negotiate hard.
- Below 50: Reject or only proceed for a very specific user constraint.

For multiple offers:

- Difference of 8+ points: treat as meaningful if confidence is medium or high.
- Difference under 8 points: treat as preference-sensitive; focus on the user's strategy and missing evidence.
- A lower-scoring offer can still win if it better fits a hard constraint or one decisive career bet.

## Red-Flag Caps

Do not let a high average score hide severe unresolved risk. Cap the recommendation at "pause / investigate" when any of these are credible and unresolved:

- Unpaid wages, delayed payroll, repeated offer changes, or refusal to put key terms in writing.
- Clear signs of abusive management, retaliation, discrimination, or unsafe working conditions.
- Misalignment between recruiter claims and written contract, equity documents, or manager statements.
- Business model, legal exposure, security posture, or ethical issue the user cannot tolerate.
- Visa, relocation, or employment-status dependency with weak written support.
- Role appears non-core, underfunded, or likely to be eliminated soon.

## Strategy Overrides

If the user states a strategy, adjust weights explicitly:

- Stability-first: raise company health, cash compensation, manager reliability, and workload predictability.
- Growth-first: raise role scope, learning density, market exposure, and resume signal.
- Cash-first: raise base, bonus certainty, benefits, severance, and liquidity; discount speculative equity.
- Immigration-first: raise written sponsorship support, legal timeline reliability, role stability, and location constraints.
- Founder or early-startup risk appetite: raise market upside, equity quality, founder quality, role leverage, and downside runway.

Always show the weights used.

## Stage-Specific Company Health Signals

For public companies, evaluate recent financial reports and market disclosures: revenue growth, margins, operating income or losses, free cash flow, cash and debt, guidance changes, segment performance, customer concentration, layoffs, buybacks or dilution, and management commentary. Do not use stock price alone as a proxy for company quality.

For startups, evaluate financing and survival odds: latest round, round type, amount, date, lead investors, valuation if available, down round or bridge signals, estimated runway, burn proxies, hiring velocity, headcount trend, customer traction, founder track record, investor quality, and whether the offered role is funded by a durable company priority.

For subsidiaries or business units, evaluate both parent-company health and unit-level priority. A strong parent does not guarantee role safety if the unit is non-core, recently reorganized, or under strategic review.
